5 Ways to Find a Planet

RADIAL VELOCITY

Watching for Wobble

1117 planets discovered

The radial velocity method detects the slight wobble of a star caused by the gravitational pull of an orbiting planet. As a planet orbits a star, the star also moves in a small circle or ellipse around their common center of mass. This movement causes the star's light to be slightly blueshifted (when moving towards us) or redshifted (when moving away from us). Astronomers can detect these tiny shifts in the star's spectrum to infer the presence and some properties of the orbiting planet.

TRANSIT

Searching for Shadows

4362 planets discovered

The transit method observes the slight dimming of a star's light as a planet passes directly in front of it from our line of sight. The amount of dimming and the duration of the transit can tell us about the planet's size and orbital period. This method has been very successful, especially with space-based telescopes like Kepler and TESS, which can continuously monitor the brightness of thousands of stars.

DIRECT IMAGING

Taking Pictures

83 planets discovered

Direct imaging involves taking pictures of exoplanets. This is challenging because planets are much fainter than their host stars. To do this, astronomers use techniques to block the star's light, such as coronagraphs. Direct imaging is most effective for finding large, young gas giant planets that are far from their stars, as they are still warm and emit more infrared light.

GRAVITATIONAL MICROLENSING

Light in a Gravity Lens

237 planets discovered

Gravitational microlensing occurs when a massive object (like a star and its planet) passes between a distant background star and us. The gravity of the foreground object bends and magnifies the light from the background star, causing it to appear brighter for a period. The specific shape of this brightening can reveal the presence and mass of a planet orbiting the lensing star.

ASTROMETRY

Measuring Positions

1 planet discovered

Astrometry involves precisely measuring the position of a star in the sky over long periods. If a star has an orbiting planet, the gravitational pull of the planet will cause the star to wobble slightly around a central point. By carefully tracking these tiny movements, astronomers can infer the presence and mass of the planet. This method is challenging due to the small angular changes involved but can be effective for finding massive planets orbiting relatively close to nearby stars.
